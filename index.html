Bird Image Classification

Classifying images of birds through CNNs

In order to challenge ourselves for one of our major class projects, my team and I decided to go above and beyond and create an image classification model for local birds of Georgia. We used both normal CNNs and a Semi-supervised approach towards CNNs to accomplish this goal.

We attempted many different models, but the best we found was the Semi-Supervised models. In these, multiple "loops" are run, where in each a Convolutional Neural Network is run on a small subset of labeled data, it is then used to predict the labels on a much larger set of unlabeled data, and those predictions with an accuracy greater than a certain threshold are then added to the training set.

This allows us to create a more iterative model than we otherwise would have with just standard CNNs, and is a great way to build models like these in "the real world."

As part of this project, my team and I wanted to explore two different approaches in session based recommendation systems: implicit context recommendation systems (ISCON), and Heterogenous Global Graph Neural Networks (HG-GNNs)

Both of these methodologies were chosen as they represent the forefront of session based recommendation systems, which utilize "sessions" – essentially windows of use - as a factor to determine which products / items to recommend. Both ISCON and HG-GNN methods complete this, but with different structures of their actual implementations.

These analyses were conducted on two different datasets: the LastFM and Reddit datasets. Both datasets were chosen as they are incredibly popular for this type of analysis.

Society of Critical Care Medicine Case Competition

Exploring correlations between survival rate and native english speakers

It has been a common belief in the medical field that patients who speak little to no english often have worse experiences in critical care situations in the United States. My team sought out to see if this was actually reflected in the data during the 2023 SCCM datathon.

As part of the competition, we had access to the MIMIC-III dataset -- one of the largest and most well known datasets of emergency room patients. This dataset has just about every piece of information you could want about patients. However, it presents some unique problems.

One of the major problems we faced was the deliberate obfuscation of patient information within the dataset. In order to meet proper medical guidelines, data must be heavily obscured so as there is no identifying information whatsoever within the dataset, as well as obscuring different key parts of the dataset, such as dates. This means we had to spend a significant amount of time cleaning our data.

The other major problem we faced as part of this process was the lack of time to conduct any major analysis. The datathon itself consisted of 1.5 days of total time, meaning that whatever model we ended up settling on for our analysis would have to be one that runs fairly quickly.

Utilizing the guidance of some of the medical doctors on my team, I was able to develop a survival model in order to see if there is a difference between native english speakers and those who are not in number of doctor check ins before passing away while in the hostpial. Overall, we found that there was no major difference between the two populations in both time it takes to die, percentage of the population which pass on, or number of check ins before passing away.

As a result of the performance of our model and our unique findings, we won the datathon!

  • Project Source: Society For Critical Care Medicine (SCCM)
  • Project type: Datathon
  • Skills / Tools Used: Python, Survival Models
